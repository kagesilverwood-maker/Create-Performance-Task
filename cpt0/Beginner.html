Points of Sail

When we are sailing, the wind is what makes our boat move. In order to move as fast and efficient as posssible, we have to know how to catch as much wind in our sails as we can. To do this we use Points of Sail (p.o.s.).


The first p.o.s. is the No-Go Zone. Think about when you walk straight into a strong gust of wind, what happens? It gets hard to walk. This is the same exact concept as the No-Go zone. In this p.o.s., we are faced into the wind and our sail is luffing (flappig like a flag) and in the center of the boat. Because the sail is luffing, it is not catching any wind which means we aren't moving. This point of sail is a 90 degree angle- or 45 degrees on each side of the wind. Remember FLAG IS BAD.


The next point of sail is the Close Haul, which is just outside of the No-Go Zone. In this point of sail, we are as close to the wind as we can get, so our sails should be pulled in to somewhere between the center and the corner of the boat.


A Close Reach is between a Close Haul and a Beam Reach, which we will learn about next. When in this p.o.s., the sails should be slightly off the corner of the boat.


A Beam Reach is perpendiculare to the wind and is the fastest p.o.s.. A good way to remembr this one is to think about a beam in a ceiling. They run horizontally and are connected to the ceiling by a vertical pole. When in this p.o.s., sails should be 1/2 of the way out. This is also the divide between upwind sailing and downwind sailing.


The next p.o.s. is a Broad Reach which is between a Beam Reach and a Run. In this point of sail, your sails should be about 3/4 of the way out.


Lastly, we have the Run which is anywhere in a 90 degree anlge that mimics the No-Go Zone. Think about if your are walking away from the wind and a huge gust comes, what happens? You are pushed forward and walking becomes easier. In this p.o.s., your sails should be all the out and the wind is just pushing you from behind.
